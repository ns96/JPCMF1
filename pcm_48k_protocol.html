PCM-48K Protocol

High-Fidelity Digital Audio Over Analog Video

1. Design Objectives

PCM-48K is a high-performance, native 48,000 Hz continuous digital audio transport protocol designed for analog video environments. It moves away from the legacy EIAJ PCM-F1 specifications to provide a efficient 1:1 hardware mapping optimized for modern capture interfaces and synchronized framebuffers.

By streamlining the data structure, PCM-48K eliminates legacy overhead while maintaining robust signal integrity. The protocol precisely maps 1,600 stereo audio samples onto 400 NTSC video lines per frame.

2. Technical Challenges

Transmitting uncompressed digital audio across analog interfaces involves two primary synchronization challenges encountered by modern capture hardware:

A. Asynchronous Framebuffer Synchronization

Software clocks typically drive digital video at exactly 30.00 Hz, while analog NTSC hardware operates at approximately 29.970 Hz. This slight timing discrepancy can result in a "torn" frame during capture, where a single buffer contains segments from two consecutive time periods.

Without proper handling, a frame tear causes a temporal discontinuity in the audio stream, resulting in periodic playback artifacts as the decoder encounters out-of-sequence data blocks.

B. Interlace Field Phase Inversion

NTSC transmits alternating odd and even fields. Standard capture hardware often weaves these two fields into a single 480-line progressive buffer. If the fields are combined out of sequence (Time=0 with Time=16ms), it results in an "interlace judder," where data on alternating lines is horizontally offset.

This offset traditionally compromises the tracking reliability of standard linear scanners, leading to significant increases in bit-error rates and CRC failures.

3. Engineering Solutions

Packet-Based Re-Sequencing

To mitigate the impact of asynchronous frame tearing, PCM-48K employs a sequence-tracking mechanism:

  • The encoder tags each horizontal data line with a 4-bit FRAME_ID.
  • The decoder analyzes the FRAME_ID of each incoming packet to detect discontinuities.
  • Packets are re-assembled into the correct chronological order within a circular jitter buffer.
  • A controlled 2-frame playback delay allows for seamless reconstruction of torn data segments before audio output, effectively eliminating synchronization artifacts.

Independent Field-Phase Tracking

To ensure reliability during field-weave mismatches, the decoding algorithm treats even and odd scanlines as independent data streams. By maintaining separate phase-tracking matrices for each field, the decoder can compensate for horizontal offsets without losing overall tracking continuity, maintaining low CRC error rates under adverse capture conditions.

Each data point occupies a 4-pixel cell. The payload is centered within the 720-pixel horizontal span, leaving substantial safety margins to account for analog blanking and overscan variability.

5. Auxiliary Features

The protocol utilizes the unused lines at the top and bottom of the video frame for diagnostic and telemetry data:

  • Integrated VU Metrics: The encoder computes peak amplitude levels in real-time, rendering active vertical volume bars at the bottom of the frame for visual monitor feedback.
  • Metadata Stream: A dedicated telemetry channel on Scanline 20 provides a 16-character ASCII metadata field. This allows for the transmission of dynamic track information and system status directly within the video signal, which is automatically parsed and displayed by the decoder.

6. Reliability and Media Constraints

Important: The PCM-48K protocol is optimized for maximum audio density and fidelity. Unlike the legacy PCM-F1 standard, it does not include P/Q Reed-Solomon error correction or data interleaving.

Hardware Compatibility

Because PCM-48K maps raw PCM data 1:1 onto video scanlines, the integrity of the audio stream depends entirely on the stability of the analog transport. Any physical signal loss, such as a tape dropout, head-switching noise, or oxide damage, will result in immediate and uncorrected audio artifacts (clicks or pops).

Consequently, the PCM-48K protocol is not suitable for physical analog media with high noise floors or mechanical instabilities, including:

  • Consumer VHS/Beta Tapes (especially at EP/SLP speeds)
  • Worn or damaged Magnetic Tape
  • Analog LaserDisc recording
  • Poorly shielded composite video cables

For applications requiring long-term archival on physical tape, the Legacy PCM-F1 Mode should be used, as it provides the necessary error correction to mitigate tape-based dropouts.
